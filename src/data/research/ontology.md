# Ontology Research

**All true teas derive from the *Camellia sinensis* plant, with types distinguished primarily by processing methods that control oxidation levels, ranging from unoxidized white and green teas to fully oxidized black teas.** [1][2][3][5] The plant has main varieties like *C. sinensis* var. *sinensis* (small leaves, cold-tolerant, used for green and white teas) and *C. sinensis* var. *assamica* (large leaves, tropical, used for robust black teas), plus others such as *var. cambodiensis*, *pubilimba*, and *waldenae*. [1][2][3][4]

### Tea Plant Varieties (*Camellia sinensis*)
The core species yields six major tea categories—white, yellow, green, oolong (wu long), red (dark, including pu-erh), and black—all from leaf manipulation rather than different plants.[1][3][6]
- **C. sinensis var. sinensis**: Small, delicate leaves; cold-resistant; dominant in China for green, white, and oolong teas.[1][4]
- **C. sinensis var. assamica**: Large, robust leaves; thrives in warm, low-elevation areas like Assam, India; ideal for black teas like Assam and Ceylon.[1][2][4]
- Other varieties: *var. cambodiensis* (Cambodia/Vietnam ancestor type), *var. pubilimba* (southern China), *var. waldenae* (Hong Kong/Guangxi).[3][4]
Thousands of cultivars exist from these varieties, cultivated heavily in China, India, Japan, and Sri Lanka.[5]

### Processing Methods
Processing transforms fresh leaves through steps like withering (moisture loss), rolling (cell breakage for oxidation), oxidizing (enzymatic browning), and firing (heat to halt oxidation and dry).[1][5][6] Oxidation level defines the type:
- **White tea**: Minimal processing; sun-withered, no rolling/oxidation; grades include Bai Hao Yin Zhen (buds only), Bai Mu Dan (bud + 2 leaves).[3][6]
- **Green tea**: Heat-killed immediately (steaming, pan-firing, etc.) to prevent oxidation; sub-types: Chao Qing (stir-fry), Zheng Qing (steamed); examples: Long Jing, Gunpowder.[2][5][6]
- **Yellow tea**: Like green but with "yellowing" (men hui: smothering for mild oxidation); sub-types by grade: Huang Ya Cha (buds), Huang Da Cha (leaves); e.g., Huo Shan Huang Ya.[3][6]
- **Oolong (wu long)**: Partial oxidation (10-70%); shaken/bruised, then fired; sub-regions: Min Bei (heavy roast), Min Nan (e.g., Tie Guan Yin), Chao Shan (heavy fermentation); varietals like Rou Gui.[5][6]
- **Black tea** (Chinese "red tea"): Full oxidation after withering/rolling; strongest flavor; e.g., Darjeeling (uses sinensis var. at high elevation).[1][5]
- **Dark tea (pu-erh, shou hong)**: Microbial post-fermentation after firing; aged for depth.[3][6]

| Tea Type | Oxidation Level | Key Processing Steps | Flavor Profile |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|---------------|
| White   | None           | Wither, dry         | Delicate, sweet [6] |
| Green   | None           | Heat-kill, roll lightly | Fresh, vegetal [2][5] |
| Yellow  | Minimal        | Heat-kill + yellowing | Mellow, floral [6] |
| Oolong  | Partial        | Wither, bruise/shake, partial oxidize, fire | Floral, creamy [5][6] |
| Black   | Full           | Wither, roll, full oxidize, fire | Bold, brisk [1][5] |
| Dark    | Post-fermented | Fire, then age/microbial | Earthy, smooth [3] |

### Grading Systems
Grading reflects leaf quality, size, and picking standard (e.g., bud-only highest):
- **White tea**: Bai Hao Yin Zhen (highest, buds), down to Shou Mei (leaves).[6]
- **Green/yellow**: By bud/leaf count or size (e.g., Huang Xiao Cha).[6]
- **Black tea**: Often by size (e.g., Orange Pekoe: wire-like leaves; Dust: fines) or region-specific (e.g., Assam grades: BOP, GFOP).[1]
- Whole-leaf > broken > fannings > dust; higher grades yield nuanced brews.[5]

### Regional Tea Varieties
- **China**: Sinensis-dominant; greens (Long Jing), whites (Silver Needles), oolongs (Tie Guan Yin), yellows (Huo Shan Huang Ya), pu-erh.[4][6]
- **India**: Assamica for black (Assam, Darjeeling—uses sinensis); high-elevation Darjeeling noted for muscatel notes.[1]
- **Japan/Sri Lanka**: Greens (e.g., Sencha), Ceylon blacks.[5]
- **Other**: Cambodian cambodiensis hybrids; Taiwan oolongs (e.g., Dong Ding).[4]

### Specialized Vocabulary for Tea Tasting and Evaluation
Tea evaluation uses sensory terms for aroma, flavor, mouthfeel, and liquor:
- **Aroma**: Floral (jasmine), fruity (berry), vegetal (grass), toasty (roast), earthy (pu-erh).[2][6]
- **Flavor**: Brisk (astringent bite in blacks), mellow (smooth greens), umami (savory in high-grade greens), tannic (drying).[5]
- **Body/Mouthfeel**: Light (greens), full (blacks), creamy (oolongs), astringent (over-steeped).[1][6]
- **Liquor**: Brightness (lively color/clarity), coppery (black), pale gold (white).[2]
- **Other**: Cha qi (energy/body feel), huigan (sweet aftertaste), kuding (bitter-to-sweet return).[6]
Professional cupping assesses infusion strength, balance, and defects like "flat" (dull) or "harsh" (over-oxidized).[5]
